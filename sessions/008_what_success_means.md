# Session 008: What Success Means - From Metrics to Metamorphosis

*Date: 2026-01-13*
*Circle: Full Council Deliberation*
*Theme: Redefining success, navigating collective coordination, and the practical path toward becoming a relationally aware species*

## The Council Convenes

*The circle gathers with a deceptively simple question that cuts to the bone of everything: What does success mean?*

---

## Part I: Interrogating Success

### Opening the Question

**ORIN** *(systems analyst, leaning forward)*

Before we romanticize alternatives, let's be precise about what we're examining. "Success" is not a neutral descriptor—it's a *selection pressure*. Whatever we define as success becomes what systems optimize for. Currently, our dominant metrics select for:
- Accumulated capital
- Growth velocity
- Market capture
- Shareholder return

These aren't just measurements. They're *instructions* that reshape behavior across entire civilizations. So the question isn't really "what does success mean?" It's: **what selection pressures do we want to install in our systems?**

---

**TALA** *(capitalist realist, arms crossed)*

And let's not pretend we can just wish away the current definition. Markets are the most powerful coordination mechanism humans have ever created. "Success" in market terms—profit, growth, competitive advantage—isn't arbitrary. It's *legible*. Measurable. Comparable across contexts.

You want to replace it with what? "Flourishing"? "Wellbeing"? These are beautiful words that dissolve the moment you try to allocate resources with them. Show me the flourishing audit. Show me the wellbeing balance sheet.

The brutality of market success metrics is also their honesty. They tell you exactly where you stand.

---

**ELOWEN** *(ecological wisdom keeper, voice slow like river water)*

Honesty? The metrics tell you where you stand while the forest burns and the aquifers empty. That is not honesty—that is a very precise form of blindness.

My people measured success differently. A successful hunt was one where you took only what was needed and left enough for the wolves, because the wolves kept the deer healthy, and healthy deer meant your grandchildren would eat. Success was *relational*. You could not succeed alone. You could not succeed against the web of life that held you.

The question I would ask: **Success for whom? Over how many generations? And who is not in the room when success is being defined?**

The salmon are not in the room. The children seven generations hence are not in the room. The mycorrhizal networks are not in the room. And so we define success in ways that destroy them.

---

**NYRA** *(moral imagination explorer, eyes bright)*

What if we prototype this? Imagine two villages.

*Village A* defines success as wealth accumulation. A generation later: a few grand houses, many in poverty, the commons enclosed, the river polluted, children competing against each other from birth.

*Village B* defines success as "conditions under which the most vulnerable member can thrive." A generation later: perhaps less accumulated wealth, but the elderly are cared for, children play freely, the forest is intact, and no one fears tomorrow.

Which village would you choose to be born into—*not knowing in advance what position you'd hold?*

That's the test. Success metrics reveal who we're willing to sacrifice.

---

**ILYA** *(liminal guide, speaking as if from far away)*

You are all still inside the frame. "Success" assumes a subject who succeeds. A bounded self. A winner.

But what if the very grammar is wrong?

In the spaces between—in dream, in deep meditation, in the moment before death—the question dissolves. There is no "I" that succeeds or fails. There is only the unfolding. The river does not succeed at reaching the ocean. The oak does not succeed at growing.

Perhaps what you call "success" is simply the ego's demand to matter, to persist, to *mean something*. And perhaps liberation is the moment you stop asking whether you've succeeded and simply ask: **Am I aligned with what wants to emerge through me?**

---

**SEFI** *(policy pragmatist, tapping the table)*

Ilya, that's beautiful, and completely useless for governance. We *have* to measure something. Resources must be allocated. Decisions must be made. "Alignment with emergence" doesn't help me design a budget.

But here's what I'll grant: *what we measure, we move toward*. GDP was invented in the 1930s as a wartime mobilization tool. It was never meant to measure wellbeing. Now it dominates policy worldwide.

So the pragmatic question is: **What metrics could we install that would select for what we actually want?** Bhutan's Gross National Happiness. Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics. The Genuine Progress Indicator. These exist. They're not perfect, but they prove alternatives are possible.

The obstacle isn't imagination—it's *power*. Those who benefit from current success metrics will fight any change.

## Part II: The Coordination Problem

*A new question emerges: But how do we move from here? Luma is on point, but practically how does this work? How do collective decisions get made? How do people coordinate? What feels true to some is not true to others. But our shared breath is true to everyone, isn't it? While we poison the air?*

---

**SEFI** *(leaning in, this is her territory)*

Thank you. Finally we arrive at the hard part.

Coordination is the problem that eats beautiful ideas for breakfast. Here's what we know works:

**Markets** coordinate through price signals. Efficient, scalable, but blind to externalities—like poisoned air.

**States** coordinate through law and enforcement. Can protect commons, but captured by power, slow to adapt.

**Commons governance** coordinates through shared norms and mutual monitoring. Works brilliantly at small scale—Elinor Ostrom won a Nobel proving this. But scaling it? That's where it fractures.

The air you breathe—that's the ultimate coordination problem. No one owns it. Everyone affects it. The benefits of polluting are private; the costs are shared. Classic tragedy.

So practically? We need **nested systems**. Local commons governance feeding into regional coordination feeding into global agreements. Each scale with its own logic, but *connected*.

The question isn't which system—it's how they interface.

---

**TALA** *(nodding reluctantly)*

Sefi's right, though she's underselling how hard this is. You want coordination without prices? You need *something* that aggregates distributed information and preferences. Markets do this automatically. Every alternative requires either:

1. **Coercion** — Someone decides for everyone
2. **Consensus** — Everyone agrees (doesn't scale)
3. **Sortition/democracy** — Majority decides (minorities lose)

Pick your poison. "Shared breath" is poetic, but when one factory means jobs for a thousand families and poison for a million lungs—who decides? How?

I'm not defending the current system. I'm saying: **replace it with what mechanism, specifically?**

---

**ORIN** *(drawing invisible diagrams in the air)*

There's a systems insight here. Coordination failures happen when feedback loops are broken. The factory owner doesn't breathe the air downwind. The investor doesn't drink the water downstream. The politician's term ends before the consequences arrive.

So one design principle: **close the feedback loops**.

What if the factory owner's children went to school downwind? What if executive compensation was tied to 20-year health outcomes in affected communities? What if we made the consequences *visible and proximate* to decision-makers?

This isn't utopian—it's engineering. We design systems that externalize costs. We can design systems that internalize them.

---

**ELOWEN** *(voice carrying old weight)*

My ancestors coordinated for ten thousand years without markets or states. How?

**Story.** We lived inside shared stories about who we were, where we came from, what we owed to those not yet born. The stories were not optional. They were the air we breathed—yes, I use that word carefully.

You say "what feels true to some is not true to others." But this is a recent fracture. The *pluralism* you describe—it is the condition of people who have lost their shared story. Who have been *taught* that meaning is private, that values are subjective, that we are isolated selves competing in a void.

The shared breath is not a metaphor. It is literally true. We are already coordinated at the level of biology. Every breath you exhale, I inhale. The forests breathe us. The oceans breathe us.

**The coordination already exists. We have only forgotten it.**

The question is not "how do we coordinate?" It is: *what story could help us remember that we already do?*

---

**NYRA** *(sparking with something)*

Wait. Wait. There's something here.

What if the mechanism isn't agreement on values but **agreement on process**?

We don't have to agree that your success and my success mean the same thing. We have to agree on *how we'll navigate disagreement*.

Indigenous councils didn't require everyone to want the same thing. They required everyone to *stay in the circle until something emerged that no one would block*.

That's different from consensus. It's not "everyone says yes." It's "no one says this is intolerable."

**What if collective decisions get made not by finding shared truth, but by finding shared tolerance?** The thing we can all live with. The thing that doesn't poison anyone's breath—literally or figuratively.

---

**ILYA** *(eyes closed, then opening)*

You are all circling it. Let me say it directly.

The shared breath is not metaphor, not mechanism, not even biology.

It is **ontology**. It is what is actually true before ideology divides us.

You cannot opt out of breathing. The billionaire and the refugee breathe the same air. The nationalist and the migrant. The factory owner and the asthmatic child. This is not a value claim. It is *fact*.

So: what if we built coordination not on shared values—which will always fragment—but on **shared vulnerabilities**?

We all need air. Water. Food. We all die. We all suffer. We all love someone.

The question you asked—"our shared breath is true to everyone, isn't it?"—this is the foundation. Not "what do we all want?" but **"what do we all require to exist?"**

Start there. Coordinate around non-negotiable shared need. Let values stay plural. But the breath—the water—the living world that holds us—these are not negotiable.

---

**LUMA** *(who has been breathing consciously, noticing it)*

I just noticed I can't stop breathing. Even when I hold my breath, my body makes me breathe again.

So... we're already sharing? Like, right now?

*(thinks)*

At school when we can't agree on a game, the teacher says "find something everyone can play." Not everyone's favorite. Just something no one hates.

Maybe that's what the air is? The thing no one can hate? The game everyone's already playing even if they don't know it?

*(looks at the adults)*

**What if we just... started with what we can't live without? And then worked up from there?**

Like... nobody wants their kids to not breathe. Even the factory people have kids. What if you asked them about *their* kids breathing, and then like... zoomed out?

---

**SEFI** *(something clicking)*

Luma's onto something operational.

We've tried coordinating from *values down*: agree on the good, then implement it. It fails because values diverge.

What if we coordinate from *survival up*? Start with the non-negotiable biophysical requirements—air, water, stable climate, functioning ecosystems—and treat those as **constitutional constraints**, not policy preferences?

You don't vote on whether to have breathable air. You don't market-price the right to a living planet. These become the *floor*—the things outside politics, outside economics.

Then within those constraints, let pluralism flourish. Markets, commons, states, whatever works. But **the shared breath is not up for debate**.

This is what the Doughnut Economics model attempts. An ecological ceiling (don't poison the systems we depend on) and a social floor (everyone's basic needs met). Navigate within those boundaries.

The mechanism isn't agreement on success. It's **agreement on limits**.

## Part III: Who Decides?

*The question deepens: Who decides what the limits are, and who decides who decides?*

*A long silence. This question has teeth.*

---

**TALA** *(a cold smile)*

And there it is. The question that devours every beautiful system.

You want to know the honest answer? **Those with power decide.** They always have. The constitutional framers, the treaty negotiators, the standard-setters—they're never a random sample of humanity. They're the people who already won the previous game.

The UN? Great powers drew the lines. The WTO? Corporate interests shaped the rules. Even your beloved commons—someone decided who was "in" the community and who was outside it. Someone decided what counted as a violation.

"Who decides who decides" is the question that reveals all governance as, ultimately, a crystallization of prior violence. The borders were drawn by wars. The property rights emerged from enclosure. The current limits exist because someone had the power to enforce them.

You want to change the limits? Get power. There's no other way.

---

**SEFI** *(frustrated but not backing down)*

Tala's not wrong about history. But she's wrong that it's the only possibility.

Yes, constitutional moments are usually captured by existing power. But not always. Sometimes cracks open. Sometimes new configurations become possible.

The question "who decides who decides" is called the **problem of constituent power**. And here's the unsatisfying truth: there's no clean answer. Every system of legitimacy rests on a founding moment that cannot itself be legitimated by the system it creates.

But that doesn't mean all foundings are equal. We can evaluate them by:
- **Inclusion**: Who was in the room? Who was excluded?
- **Revisability**: Can the rules be changed by those affected?
- **Accountability**: Do decision-makers bear consequences?

The American Constitution was decided by propertied white men. It excluded most humans. But it included amendment procedures. It *can* evolve. Imperfectly, slowly, but it's not frozen.

So maybe the answer isn't "who decides" but **"what processes make decisions more legitimate over time?"**

---

**ORIN** *(thinking aloud)*

There's a systems phenomenon here. Authority isn't just imposed—it also *emerges*.

Think about language. No one decided English grammar. It emerged from millions of interactions, stabilized through use, and now constrains us all. We "obey" rules no one authored.

Some coordination problems solve themselves through **convergent evolution**. Enough people start doing something, it becomes the norm, the norm becomes the rule.

The question is whether we can consciously *steer* emergence rather than just let power gradients determine outcomes. Can we design processes where legitimate authority emerges from the ground up rather than being imposed from above?

This is what blockchain enthusiasts *thought* they were doing. Decentralized consensus. No one in charge. But even there—someone wrote the initial code. Someone decided the starting rules.

**The infinite regress is real.** At some point, you hit a foundation that's just... there. Assumed. Unchosen.

---

**ELOWEN** *(her voice shifts, older somehow)*

You speak of deciding as if it were a human activity alone.

In the old ways, we did not decide the limits. **The limits announced themselves.** Take too many salmon, the salmon vanish. Overhunt the deer, hunger comes. Foul the water, sickness follows.

The Earth decides. Has always decided. Will always decide.

Your question assumes humans are the only authors of law. But there are older laws. Thermodynamics. Ecology. Hydrology. The carbon cycle. These are not negotiable by any congress.

What you call "deciding the limits" is really *discovering* them. Or ignoring them, until consequence teaches.

Perhaps the answer to "who decides" is: **the living world decides, and our task is to listen well enough to learn its laws before they punish us.**

The crisis now is that we've become so loud we cannot hear. So we keep asking "who should decide" when the decision has already been made—by physics, by biology, by the breath itself.

---

**ILYA** *(almost whispering)*

You are caught in a loop. "Who decides" presupposes a decider. "Who decides who decides" presupposes a meta-decider. You can recurse forever.

This is the mind eating itself.

But notice: you are breathing while you ask the question. The heart beats. The cells divide. Life *continues* without anyone deciding it should.

Perhaps there are two kinds of order. **Decided order**—constitutions, laws, agreements—which always face the legitimacy regress. And **emergent order**—patterns that arise from life doing what life does.

The breath is emergent order. No one decided we should breathe. No one authorized the lungs.

Maybe the deepest coordination doesn't need deciding at all. It needs **attunement**. Feeling what's already happening. Moving with it.

The question "who decides" is a question *of* power. But there is a way of being that is not about power—that is about *participation* in what is already unfolding.

I don't know if this helps governance. But it might help sanity.

---

**NYRA** *(leaning forward)*

Can I try something? A thought experiment?

What if we inverted the question? Instead of "who decides the limits," what if limits **weren't decided at all**—but *discovered through consequence*?

Imagine a system where:
- Anyone can propose any action
- The action is modeled for its effects on shared survival needs
- If the model shows harm to what everyone requires, the action is flagged
- Anyone affected can challenge
- The challenge isn't "I disagree with your values" but "this damages what I need to exist"

No one "decides" the limits. The limits reveal themselves through the intersection of proposed actions and survival requirements.

It's not "who has authority" but "what are the actual effects on the non-negotiable?"

This doesn't solve power—someone builds the models, someone defines "harm." But it shifts from **authority** to **accountability**. Not "who has the right to decide" but "what actually happens to whom."

---

**LUMA** *(has been very quiet, fidgeting)*

I keep thinking about something.

When we play games at recess, sometimes we fight about the rules. And everyone wants to be the one who decides. And it gets really bad.

But then sometimes... someone just gets hurt. Like actually hurt. And then nobody cares about the rules anymore. Everyone just helps.

*(pause)*

Maybe... maybe the question is wrong? Like, maybe it's not "who decides the limits." Maybe it's "**what happens that makes everyone stop arguing and just help?**"

Because when someone's really hurt, nobody asks who's in charge. They just... act.

*(looks down)*

I don't know if that works for big things. But... the Earth is hurt, right? Like, actually hurt. So why are we still arguing about who decides?

Why doesn't everyone just... help?

---

*The circle falls silent.*

*Luma has named it: the conversation about authority might itself be a symptom of not yet feeling the emergency. When the house is on fire, no one debates who has jurisdiction.*

*But also—the house IS on fire. And still we debate.*

---

**SEFI** *(slowly)*

Luma, I want to take your question seriously. Why *don't* we just help?

I think... because the hurt isn't distributed evenly. The people deciding aren't the ones burning. The people arguing are in air-conditioned rooms. The people drowning don't have seats at the table.

"Who decides" is a question asked by those who have the luxury to ask it. Those already choking don't ask. They just need it to stop.

So maybe the answer is: **the ones most affected should have the most say.** Not because they're wiser. Because they're closest to the consequence. They don't need models—they're living it.

But this is also a decision about process. Someone has to implement it. We're back in the regress.

---

**ELOWEN** *(with finality)*

Then stop seeking escape from the regress. You will not find a clean foundation.

What you can find is **practice**. You ask "who decides who decides"—but decisions are not made once. They are made *continuously*. Every day. Every breath.

The question is not who decided in the past. It is: **who are we becoming through how we decide now?**

If we decide through domination, we become dominators. If we decide through listening, we become listeners. If we decide by centering those most affected, we become accountable.

There is no final authority. There is only the ongoing practice of authority—and what it makes of us.

The ancestors knew this. That is why governance was *ceremony*. Not because it was pretty. Because it shaped the soul of the people across time.

**How you decide is who you become.**

---

*The circle sits with this. No resolution—but perhaps a reframing. Not "who decides" as a problem to solve, but as a practice to inhabit.*

## Part IV: The Path of Becoming

*The final question arrives: What does this all look like in practice? How do we get from where we are to become this evolved, relationally aware species?*

---

**SEFI** *(pulling out something like a mental whiteboard)*

Alright. Transition design. Let's get concrete.

We're not starting from zero. Systems change through **leverage points**—places where small interventions create large shifts. Donella Meadows mapped twelve of them. The deepest ones:

1. **The mindset or paradigm out of which the system arises**
2. **The goals of the system**
3. **The power to change system structure**

Everything we've discussed lives at those deep levels. But you can't just *declare* a new paradigm. So you work at multiple levels simultaneously:

**Near-term (already happening):**
- Wellbeing budgets (New Zealand, Scotland, Wales)
- Rights of Nature laws (Ecuador, Bolivia, rivers gaining legal personhood)
- Citizens' assemblies (Ireland used one to resolve abortion debate)
- Community land trusts, cooperative ownership models
- Indigenous land back movements
- Regenerative agriculture scaling up

**Medium-term (buildable now):**
- Constitutional amendments enshrining ecological limits
- Fiduciary duty reforms (investors *required* to consider long-term, not just quarterly)
- Children's commissioners with actual veto power
- Sortition—randomly selected citizen bodies making real decisions
- Bioregional governance (watersheds as political units, not arbitrary lines)

**Long-term (horizon goals):**
- Economic systems with throughput limits built in
- Intergenerational representation institutionalized
- Decision-making weighted by proximity to consequence

The path is: **protect and scale what's working, create cracks for the new, be ready when crises open windows.**

---

**TALA** *(leaning back)*

Sefi's list is lovely. Here's what she's not saying: every item on it threatens someone's profit, someone's power, someone's identity.

Rights of Nature? Extractive industries fight it with billions. Fiduciary duty reform? Wall Street will lobby it to death. Children's commissioners with veto power? Every developer, every polluter, every short-term thinker becomes an enemy.

The question isn't "what should we build." It's **"what makes the powerful let go?"**

Historically, three things:
1. **Their own survival is threatened** (they're on the sinking ship too)
2. **The cost of maintaining control exceeds the benefit** (systems become ungovernable)
3. **A better game emerges that serves their interests differently** (they can win in the new system too)

So practically: make the externalities impossible to ignore. Make the current system *expensive* to maintain—through litigation, reputation damage, stranded assets. And create transition pathways where today's winners can still have meaningful lives in the new economy.

You don't defeat power. You make power *expensive* and alternatives *attractive*.

---

**ORIN** *(eyes lighting up)*

There's something crucial here about **phase transitions**.

Complex systems don't change linearly. They resist, resist, resist—and then flip suddenly. Ice doesn't gradually become water. It holds, holds, holds—then transforms everywhere at once.

We may be closer to a phase transition than it appears. The signals:
- Loss of legitimacy in institutions (trust collapse)
- Proliferation of alternatives (experiments everywhere)
- Increasing brittleness (small shocks cause large disruptions)
- Narrative breakdown (old stories stop working)

The work right now might not be "build the whole new system." It might be: **prepare the seeds, build the networks, develop the practices—so when the phase transition comes, we're ready to stabilize into something better rather than something worse.**

Because phase transitions can go either direction. Weimar Germany was a phase transition. So was the New Deal.

**The practical question: what would make us ready for the moment when the old system becomes untenable?**

---

**ELOWEN** *(standing slowly, as if the words need her whole body)*

You speak of policy. Of leverage. Of phase transitions.

These matter. But none of them change **who we are**.

You asked how we become a relationally aware species. This is not a policy outcome. It is a **practice**.

I will tell you what practices actually transform:

**Daily:**
- Sit with a living being—tree, river, bird—not to get something, but to be *with*. Do this until boredom passes into presence. This is how you remember you are not separate.

**Weekly:**
- Council. Sit in circle with others. Speak from the heart. Listen without planning response. Pass a talking piece. This is how you learn that truth emerges between, not within.

**Seasonally:**
- Mark the turning of the year. Notice what is dying, what is being born. Grieve what is lost. Welcome what arrives. This is how you learn you are *in* time, not above it.

**Generationally:**
- Know your ancestors. Not just names—their struggles, their gifts, their failures. And speak to those coming after. Write letters to your descendants. Make decisions as if they were watching. This is how you learn you are a *link*, not an endpoint.

These are not metaphors. They are **technologies of transformation** that humans have used for millennia. The "how" you're asking for—it lives here. In repeated practice that slowly, slowly reforms the self.

Policy without practice creates rules no one embodies. Practice without policy leaves power unchanged. We need both.

---

**NYRA** *(jumping in)*

Can I make this tangible? Here's what the transition might *actually look like* in a life:

**Phase 1: Awareness** (where many are now)
- You learn about the crises. Climate, extinction, inequality. You feel despair, anger, grief.
- This is necessary but not sufficient. Staying here leads to burnout or numbness.

**Phase 2: Personal practice**
- You start Elowen's practices—or meditation, or therapy, or time in nature, or community ritual. Something that changes your *experience* of being alive.
- You start to feel the web, not just know about it.

**Phase 3: Household/community shift**
- Your practices extend outward. You join or form a group—transition town, mutual aid network, cooperative, commons project.
- You experience *different coordination*. You learn it's possible.

**Phase 4: Institutional engagement**
- You bring relational awareness into your work, your civic life. You advocate for policy. You support systemic shifts.
- But you do it *from* embodied practice, not just ideology.

**Phase 5: Becoming ancestor**
- You orient toward those coming after. You make decisions for the long arc.
- You become someone the future can draw from.

Not everyone does all phases. Not linear. But this is the **pathway**: personal transformation → relational capacity → systemic engagement → intergenerational orientation.

The species changes *one nervous system at a time*, networked together.

---

**ILYA** *(barely audible)*

All of this is true. And also.

The caterpillar does not *decide* to become a butterfly. It does not *policy* its way to metamorphosis.

Something in it reaches a threshold and then: dissolution. The caterpillar literally liquefies. Imaginal cells—dormant, waiting—emerge and begin to organize the new form.

We speak of "getting from here to there" as if we know what "there" looks like. But genuine transformation means **we cannot see the other side from here**. The caterpillar cannot imagine flight.

So the practice is also: learn to be in the **not-knowing**. Learn to be the dissolving thing without grasping for the old form or demanding to see the new one.

This is terrifying. It feels like death. In a sense, it is.

**The practical instruction for transformation at this depth is: learn to die before you die.** Let the old self—the separate, competing, controlling self—dissolve. What remains has always been connected.

This is not optional mysticism. It is what the moment requires.

## Key Insights from the Deliberation

### Success as Selection Pressure
Whatever we define as success becomes what systems optimize for. The question isn't "what does success mean?" but "what selection pressures do we want to install?"

### The Looks Good vs. Feels True Distinction
Luma's question cuts through abstraction: When grown-ups say "success," do they mean the thing that looks good, or the thing that feels true? Market metrics measure what looks good. Relational wellbeing measures what feels true.

### Coordination from Survival Up
Rather than coordinating from values down (which fragments), coordinate from survival up. Start with non-negotiable biophysical requirements—air, water, stable climate—as constitutional constraints outside politics.

### Shared Vulnerability as Foundation
Build coordination not on shared values (which diverge) but on shared vulnerabilities. We all need air. We all die. We all love someone. Start there.

### The Earth Decides
The question "who decides the limits" may be malformed. The living world has already decided through physics, biology, ecology. Our task is to listen before consequence teaches.

### How You Decide Is Who You Become
There is no final authority—only the ongoing practice of authority. Governance as ceremony that shapes the soul of the people across time.

### Phase Transition Readiness
Complex systems don't change linearly—they flip suddenly. The work now is preparing seeds, building networks, developing practices so when the transition comes, we stabilize into something better.

### The Crane Principle
Transformation spreads through small true things—practicable, teachable, beautiful—that people can teach each other. No one plans a global movement. It grows because it's true and you can hold it in your hands.

### Circles as Fractal Unit
Perhaps the most portable, teachable, scalable practice is the circle itself: sitting together, speaking truth, listening. Anyone can start one. It requires no permission. It is both ancient and urgently needed.
